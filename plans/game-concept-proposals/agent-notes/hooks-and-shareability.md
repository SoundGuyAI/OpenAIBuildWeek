## Pitch cards

### 1. Loop Engineer

- **Premise:** An edutainment management game where the player employs an NPC
  mentor who teaches agentic software development across the software
  development life cycle (SDLC) and SDLR by turning loop engineering into a
  visible, playable system.
- **Hook:** Hire a mentor. Ship through SDLC and SDLR.
- **Trailer/demo moment:** A hopeless task arrives as a giant red card. The
  mentor NPC pauses the chaos, frames the software task across SDLC and SDLR,
  and demonstrates how loop engineering turns a rough request into a plan,
  bounded specialist work, tests, evidence, and the next improvement. On the
  final pass, twenty scattered errors collapse into one verified result and the
  workshop transforms from clutter to flow.
- **Spectator-readable beats:** `confusing software request` -> `NPC connects
  SDLC + SDLR` -> `player builds the loop` -> `specialist agents work in
  parallel` -> `tests and evidence drive the next iteration`.
- **Optional comedic reversal:** The mentor gives excellent advice but is an
  appallingly dramatic employee: every failed test triggers a tiny emergency
  siren, a hard hat, and the line, “Excellent. We found information.”
- **Audience position:** Hackathon builders, students, technical teams, and
  judges looking for an AI-native way to learn agentic software development
  across SDLC and SDLR with a clear before/after.
- **Pitch line:** “It makes SDLC and SDLR loop engineering a playable team sport.”
- **Scores:** instant comprehension **5/5**; shareability **4/5**; streamer or
  spectator value **5/5**; hackathon pitch strength **5/5**.
- **Flag:** Name and premise are mandatory and should remain unchanged. Keep
  `SDLR` as the owner's framework term and market the curriculum through
  concrete loop-engineering actions, tests, evidence, feedback, and iteration.

### 2. Shadow Locksmith

- **Hook:** Forge impossible keys from ordinary shadows.
- **Trailer/demo moment:** The player rotates a teapot, ladder, and rubber duck
  until their combined shadow becomes an ornate key; the shadow peels off the
  wall, turns solid, and opens a giant moonlit lock.
- **Spectator-readable beats:** `recognizable junk` -> `wild silhouette` ->
  `perfect alignment flash` -> `shadow becomes object` -> `door reveals vista`.
- **Optional comedic reversal:** The final “ancient key” is unmistakably the
  profile of a duck wearing a crown.
- **Audience position:** Puzzle fans, mixed-reality-curious players, families,
  and judges who reward a mechanic readable from a single GIF.
- **Scores:** instant **5/5**; shareability **5/5**; spectator **5/5**; pitch
  **5/5**.
- **Flag:** No naming concern. Never describe it merely as “a perspective
  puzzle”; lead with shadows becoming physical keys.

### 3. Museum of Unfinished Weather

- **Hook:** Finish storms trapped inside museum exhibits.
- **Trailer/demo moment:** The player reaches into a glass diorama, pulls a
  ribbon of wind around a model town, places a thunderhead, then catches the
  resulting lightning in a display placard as the exhibit comes alive.
- **Spectator-readable beats:** `dead exhibit` -> `hands sculpt weather` ->
  `town reacts` -> `storm reaches beautiful equilibrium` -> `gallery applauds`.
- **Optional comedic reversal:** A dignified curator insists the indoor tornado
  is “within conservation guidelines” while being slowly rolled away.
- **Audience position:** Art-game players, cozy puzzle fans, science museums,
  educators, and visually focused hackathon judges.
- **Scores:** instant **4/5**; shareability **5/5**; spectator **5/5**; pitch
  **5/5**.
- **Flag:** Excellent evocative title, but long for an app tile. “Weather
  Museum” is shorter and much more generic; keep the full title for pitching.

### 4. Pocket Kaiju Tailor

- **Hook:** Dress giant monsters for tiny disasters.
- **Trailer/demo moment:** A city-sized kaiju kneels beside the player’s tiny
  tailoring table. The player snaps on oven mitts, a lightning-proof cape, and
  enormous roller skates; the monster then elegantly saves a burning bakery.
- **Spectator-readable beats:** `disaster card appears` -> `inspect expressive
kaiju` -> `choose absurd gear` -> `giant runway turn` -> `solution succeeds or
hilariously backfires`.
- **Optional comedic reversal:** Fashion score and rescue score disagree. The
  safest outfit is devastatingly unfashionable, and the kaiju knows it.
- **Audience position:** Broad casual audiences, creature-customization fans,
  families, streamers, and judges wanting character plus tactile scale.
- **Scores:** instant **5/5**; shareability **5/5**; spectator **5/5**; pitch
  **5/5**.
- **Flag:** Strong name and hook. Protect the contrast between “pocket tailor”
  and “giant client”; it is the marketing image.

### 5. Choir of One

- **Hook:** Conduct your past selves into harmony.
- **Trailer/demo moment:** The player performs one simple note, rewinds, and
  watches a luminous echo repeat it. Three more recorded selves join until the
  room becomes a spatial choir and a shattered bridge rebuilds on the chord.
- **Spectator-readable beats:** `solo action` -> `time echo appears` -> `layers
multiply` -> `player conducts the ensemble` -> `world resolves on harmony`.
- **Optional comedic reversal:** One echo stubbornly preserves an early mistake
  and becomes the accidental percussion section.
- **Audience position:** Music-game players, creators, loop-station fans, and
  audiences drawn to emotional rather than comic spectacle.
- **Scores:** instant **4/5**; shareability **5/5**; spectator **5/5**; pitch
  **5/5**.
- **Flag:** Strong title. The trailer must show the first duplicate within five
  seconds or the time-loop mechanic remains abstract.

### 6. Gravity Graffiti

- **Trailer/demo moment:** The player sprays a neon arrow up a skyscraper; cars,

  spectacular chain reaction.
- **Spectator-readable beats:** `blocked route` -> `paint directional force` ->
  `world tilts locally` -> `objects cascade` -> `player repaints the mess`.
- **Optional comedic reversal:** A smug tutorial apple keeps obeying every new
  gravity rule long after it has stopped being useful.
- **Audience position:** Physics-toy fans, speedrunners, creative problem
  solvers, short-form video audiences, and technically minded judges.
- **Scores:** instant **5/5**; shareability **5/5**; spectator **5/5**; pitch
  **5/5**.
- **Flag:** Strong hook. “Regret some choices” promises playful failure; the

### 7. The Last Teacup

- **Trailer/demo moment:** Seen at dollhouse scale, a perfect tea party begins.
  The room suddenly tilts, a cat paw enters like a natural disaster, and the
  player steadies a single sloshing cup while rescuing tiny guests.
- **Spectator-readable beats:** `precious calm tableau` -> `giant hazard enters`
  -> `player catches wobbling objects` -> `tea nearly spills` -> `impossible
toast succeeds`.
- **Optional comedic reversal:** The guests remain unfailingly polite while the
  apocalypse rearranges the cake stand.
- **Audience position:** Cozy-game fans, dexterity players, families, and
  spectators who enjoy miniature scale and escalating physical comedy.
- **Scores:** instant **5/5**; shareability **5/5**; spectator **5/5**; pitch
  **5/5**.
- **Flag:** Title alone could imply a narrative adventure. Pair it everywhere
  with the hook or a hero image of the giant cat paw.

## Portfolio guidance

### Best cold-room hackathon pitches

1. **Loop Engineer** — strongest relevance to an agent-building event; the NPC
   mentor and visible loop make agentic software development across SDLC and
   SDLR concrete, demonstrable, and spectator-readable.
2. **Shadow Locksmith** — clearest one-GIF mechanic and immediate spatial payoff.
3. **Pocket Kaiju Tailor** — strongest character, scale, comedy, and merch-like
   visual identity.
4. **Gravity Graffiti** — clearest systemic toy with built-in failure clips.
5. **Starwhale Post Office** — strongest world reveal and family-friendly image.

### Best trailer structure across concepts

Use the same five-shot grammar without making the games feel identical:

1. **Promise in one image:** show the fantasy and the player-scale relationship.
2. **One input, one response:** make the core verb readable with no narration.
3. **Escalation:** add one complication spectators can predict before it lands.
4. **Hero reversal:** transform the whole space, creature, system, or story.
5. **Result card:** one bold score/lesson/outcome plus a one-action retry.

### Shareability guardrails

- A clip should contain a visible setup, player-caused turn, and outcome within
  8-12 seconds.
- Put expressive reactions in the world, not only in HUD text. Giant faces,
  crowds, lighting, geometry, and synchronized motion survive muted playback.
- Preserve player agency in failure clips. Reversible, legible chaos is funnier
  and more shareable than unexplained punishment.
- Do not force jokes into **Choir of One**, **Signal Fireflies**, or **The Room
  That Remembers**. Their quieter emotional contrast improves the portfolio.
- For every XR hero shot, capture an equivalent desktop/mobile shot showing the
  same decision and outcome; market parity as intentional, not as a fallback.
