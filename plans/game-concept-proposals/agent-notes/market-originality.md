# Market and Originality Research: WebXR Game Territories

**Role:** Market/originality specialist
**Date:** 2026-07-20
**Scope:** Generate at least 30 candidate territories, recommend a differentiated set of 20, identify recognizable comparables, and document clone-avoidance rules.
**Decision status:** Research input for the orchestrator; not a claim of global novelty, legal clearance, trademark clearance, or final product selection.

## Executive recommendation

The strongest portfolio is not twenty variations on “VR hands touching objects.” It is twenty sharply separated fantasies whose primary verbs, pacing, social structures, tones, and uses of 3D space are legible in a silent ten-second clip.

The recommended set below deliberately spans:

- instruction and orchestration, inspection, conducting, excavation, acoustic reasoning, strategy, movement, cooperative physics, social deduction, diplomacy, competitive steering, wordplay, sports reconstruction, audio exploration, topology, gardening, and racing;
- solo, cooperative, competitive, asymmetric, party, and pass-and-play structures;
- seated desk scale, tabletop scale, room scale, panoramic space, vertical space, spherical space, and layered diorama space;
- comic, contemplative, mysterious, cozy, satirical, educational, dramatic, and high-energy tones.

The mandatory concept **Loop Engineer** teaches agentic software development through loop engineering across both **SDLC** and **SDLR**. The player employs an NPC mentor who makes planning, delegation, iteration, evidence review, and delivery visible. In this note, SDLC means **software development life cycle**; **SDLR is retained exactly as the project's framework term and is intentionally left unexpanded**. The originality assessment concerns the playable teaching structure, not a claim that either framework term is itself novel.

## Market read and opportunity filter

### Visible crowded neighborhoods

Current storefronts and showcases make several VR patterns immediately recognizable: rhythm striking, firearms/action, social hand-locomotion, sports/minigolf, tabletop fantasy combat, physics-job comedy, and static 3D assembly. These are useful comparables because audiences understand them quickly, but they are weak originality positions unless the new game changes the governing interaction rather than only its theme.

The scan therefore treats the following as “red-ocean defaults”:

1. Beat-matched objects arriving on rails.
2. A shooting gallery with a new enemy skin.
3. Physics comedy built mainly from throwing breakable props.
4. A miniature dungeon with cards, dice, and combat turns.
5. A static object-assembly puzzle.
6. Arm-swing locomotion plus tag or infection.
7. A conventional escape room whose puzzles merely happen to be in VR.
8. A desktop game copied into a floating panel with motion-controller cursors.

### WebXR-specific white space

The best WebXR hackathon concepts exploit qualities that survive across headset, mouse/keyboard, and touch:

- **One-link participation:** a spectator or second player can join without a native-store installation.
- **Equivalent verbs, not identical controls:** grab in XR, drag with a pointer, and direct-manipulate by touch can all express the same decision.
- **Readable spatial state:** the world itself communicates status, reducing dependence on text-heavy HUDs.
- **Short-session spectacle:** a concept should communicate in seconds and complete a satisfying loop in roughly 3–12 minutes.
- **Comfort-first staging:** most proposals can work seated or standing without forced smooth locomotion.
- **A strong non-XR product:** desktop/mobile is a first-class expression, not a reduced spectator mode.

### Originality test used

Each recommendation passed four filters:

1. **Silent GIF test:** the fantasy and central verb are understandable before reading copy.
2. **Mechanic sentence test:** its differentiation can be stated without relying on lore or art style.
3. **Clone subtraction test:** removing the new theme does not expose the unchanged loop of the nearest comparable.
4. **Cross-device equivalence test:** XR, mouse, and touch players make materially equivalent choices.

## Recommended concept analyses

### 1. Loop Engineer

**Immediate visual:** A circular miniature workshop surrounds the player. Tiny specialist agents move task capsules between glowing stations while an NPC wearing an “Employee / Mentor” badge points at bottlenecks, stamps review cards, and freezes the loop for teachable moments.

**Premise and loop:** The player employs an NPC who teaches agentic software development through loop engineering across **SDLC and SDLR**. A client request arrives; the player asks the employed mentor for guidance, specifies an observable outcome, hires or assigns specialist agents, delegates bounded tasks, watches their artifacts circulate, intervenes when loops stall, and reviews evidence before shipping. Each round ends with the NPC explaining how the orchestration affected the development and review loops and why the result succeeded or failed.

**Terminology boundary:** **SDLC** refers to the software development life cycle. **SDLR** is a project-owned framework term and remains unexpanded here. The concept should show how loop engineering supports both through observable goals, bounded agent roles, artifact handoffs, verification, feedback, and iteration. Do not substitute an unrelated educational expansion or invent a mnemonic for SDLR.

**Closest comparables:** _Human Resource Machine_ and _7 Billion Humans_ teach programming through worker metaphors; _while True: learn()_ uses a machine-learning/business wrapper. These are adjacent educational-automation references, not matches for the complete Loop Engineer structure.

**How not to clone:** Do not make the agents execute pseudo-code blocks, do not use a worker-swarm grid, and do not imitate a production agent dashboard or terminal. The challenge is managerial judgment: define acceptance criteria, choose roles, set write scopes, decide when to parallelize, inspect evidence, and recover from failure.

**Differentiated interaction:** The player hires the teacher, yet the employee trains the employer. Learning happens inside an embodied, observable delivery loop where agent ownership, dependencies, evidence, and review are physical objects. No close match for this exact combination was identified among the retained comparables in this bounded scan.

**Cross-device expression:** XR players place role tokens and connect workstations by hand. Desktop players drag cards and cables in an isometric workshop. Mobile players tap agents, swipe task capsules, and pinch the loop. All versions make the same planning and review decisions.

**Originality risk:** **Medium-low**, provided the art avoids office-dashboard realism and the curriculum focuses on orchestration rather than coding puzzles.

### 2. Bureau of Impossible Doorways

**Immediate visual:** A nervous cloud-person waits behind a doorway whose ocean horizon is upside down. The player rotates the frame, checks the traveler's shadow against a destination stamp, and either opens the portal or rings a huge red bell.

**Core loop:** Inspect a traveler, compare their physical anomalies with destination rules, manipulate the doorway until its geometry is valid, decide admit/quarantine/redirect, then survive consequences that alter later rules.

**Closest comparables:** _Papers, Please_ for timed inspection and moral choices; _Job Simulator_ for embodied desk work.

**How not to clone:** Avoid passports, totalitarian parody, wages, and line-by-line document comparison. Rules should be visible in perspective, gravity, reflection, shadow, scale, and portal continuity.

**Differentiated interaction:** The checkpoint's architecture is the evidence. A rule such as “visitors to Tuesday must cast tomorrow's shadow” requires spatial experimentation, not text matching.

**Originality risk:** **Medium-low**. The inspection loop is familiar; the impossible-geometry evidence must dominate.

### 3. Shadow Smugglers

**Immediate visual:** One player holds a lantern over a tabletop city while another tiny character runs only inside the safe shadow. Tilting a cardboard dragon creates a moving bridge and also hides the runner from searchlights.

**Core loop:** A light-shaper positions props and light sources; an infiltrator traverses the resulting silhouettes, steals one object, and escapes before the light cycle changes.

**Closest comparables:** _Carly and the Reaperman_ for asymmetric VR/desktop cooperation and _Acron_ for cross-device role asymmetry.

**How not to clone:** Do not have the XR player place ordinary platforms for a conventional platformer. Shadows must simultaneously define walkable space, visibility, and enemy perception.

**Differentiated interaction:** One shared light field is terrain, stealth meter, and communication language. Roles can rotate each round so mobile/desktop is never merely spectating.

**Originality risk:** **Low-medium**. Comparable social structure exists, but the three-purpose shadow system is uncommon.

### 4. Choir of Currents

**Immediate visual:** Bioluminescent creatures drift around the player. A hand sweep bends a visible current, carrying one creature through a chord ring; the entire reef changes color as harmony resolves.

**Core loop:** Listen to a fractured phrase, shape two or three persistent fluid streams, place creatures into complementary paths, then conduct the living arrangement through a short performance.

**Closest comparables:** _Maestro_ and rhythm-action games such as _Beat Saber_.

**How not to clone:** No notes should travel toward hit zones, and success should not depend on saber-like impact timing. The player composes a stable flow field and then makes expressive adjustments.

**Differentiated interaction:** Continuous current sculpting determines pitch, timing, and group motion at once. It is closer to gardening a song than hitting one.

**Originality risk:** **Medium-low** if the fluid-field planning is more important than beat accuracy.

### 5. Museum of Unfinished Weather

**Immediate visual:** A glass museum case contains half a thunderstorm: rain hangs motionless above a desert while a tornado ends in a neat ribbon. Pulling one cloud layer forward restarts lightning across the case.

**Core loop:** Read a curator's incomplete exhibit note, rotate layered climate systems, connect moisture/heat/pressure paths, run the weather, and refine until the diorama tells the intended story.

**Closest comparables:** _Puzzling Places_ and _Cubism_ for calm spatial reasoning.

**How not to clone:** Avoid assembling predetermined solid pieces. Weather components must interact dynamically, support several stable outcomes, and reveal causal chains.

**Differentiated interaction:** A transparent diorama lets the player edit atmospheric layers at different depths, then watch emergent weather validate the hypothesis.

**Originality risk:** **Low** in the reviewed set; the main risk is simulation scope rather than market similarity.

### 6. Archaeology of Tomorrow

**Immediate visual:** The player brushes dust from a fossilized smartphone and the ruin flickers into three incompatible futures. Digging toward one makes the others physically crumble.

**Core loop:** Survey a site, choose where to excavate, preserve or sacrifice evidence, connect artifacts into a future-history theory, and publish a conclusion that transforms the final reconstruction.

**Closest comparables:** _Return of the Obra Dinn_ for evidence-led reconstruction and archaeology games for brushing/excavation.

**How not to clone:** Avoid frozen death tableaux, insurance-style identity deduction, or a single canonical sequence. The irreversible act of uncovering one possible future must erase alternatives.

**Differentiated interaction:** Knowledge has a physical cost. The player cannot collect every clue and must reason about which uncertainty to destroy.

**Originality risk:** **Low-medium**. Narrative branching is familiar; destructive future archaeology is the defensible hook.

### 7. Dream Lost & Found

**Immediate visual:** Three dreamers argue over a suitcase that becomes a staircase when one describes it and fills with rain when another touches it. The clerk pins memory fragments around it to identify the rightful dream.

**Core loop:** Interview claimants, test an object's mutable properties, arrange memory evidence, choose a recipient, then see the object alter that dreamer's miniature world.

**Closest comparables:** _Wilmot's Warehouse_ for visual classification and _Strange Horticulture_ for inference from descriptions.

**How not to clone:** Do not make it a speed-sorting warehouse or botanical catalog. Every item's category should depend on perspective, language, and transformation.

**Differentiated interaction:** Testimony changes the evidence in real time. Classification is not finding a hidden fixed label; it is deciding which remembered identity is most coherent.

**Originality risk:** **Medium-low**.

### 8. Echo Locksmith

**Immediate visual:** A glowing pulse travels from the player's hand, wraps around pillars, and returns as colored rings. Sliding one wall changes the returning chord and opens a lock with no keyhole.

**Core loop:** Emit a probe sound, observe its path and echo, reshape reflectors/absorbers, match a target resonance, then perform a final rhythmic knock through the solved acoustic space.

**Closest comparables:** _The Room VR_ for tactile mystery puzzles and spatial-audio puzzle experiences.

**How not to clone:** Avoid ornate mechanical locks, hidden keys, and inventory riddles. Every puzzle should be explainable through path length, reflection, occlusion, damping, or resonance.

**Differentiated interaction:** The player edits architecture to tune sound. Visualized echoes make the concept playable without perfect hearing, while headphones add depth.

**Originality risk:** **Low** in the reviewed set.

### 9. One-Minute Civilizations

**Immediate visual:** A civilization sprouts across a tabletop, discovers fire, builds a tower, floods, and becomes ruins in sixty seconds. The player catches one glowing invention before the next society grows over it.

**Core loop:** Place one environmental influence, observe a rapid generation, rescue one idea, seed it into the next generation, and pursue a multi-generation objective through inheritance rather than direct control.

**Closest comparables:** _Townscaper_, god games, and tabletop strategy such as _Demeo_ at the level of spatial presentation.

**How not to clone:** No combat turns, unit micromanagement, or unconstrained city painting. The central constraint is choosing one cultural memory under severe time compression.

**Differentiated interaction:** The emotional and strategic cost of preservation is visible every minute. Civilizations become layers of physical history around the player.

**Originality risk:** **Low-medium**.

### 10. Gravity Graffiti Couriers

**Immediate visual:** A tiny courier reaches a wall. The player paints a bright arrow up its face; loose parcels and the courier fall sideways, racing along the new “floor.”

**Core loop:** Survey a compact route, paint limited gravity fields, launch the courier, repaint one or two sections in real time, and deliver fragile parcels with momentum intact.

**Closest comparables:** _Portal_ and gravity-oriented traversal puzzles.

**How not to clone:** No portal pairs, first-person test chambers, or direct avatar jumping. The player paints vector fields onto a miniature environment and manages moving cargo.

**Differentiated interaction:** Graffiti is a local physics instruction. Multiple painted surfaces can form loops, slingshots, and orientation-dependent shortcuts.

**Originality risk:** **Medium-low**.

### 11. Haunted Moving Company

**Immediate visual:** A cheerful sofa grows spider legs when lifted from the wrong side, while a grandfather clock sulks and becomes impossibly heavy unless another player hums near it.

**Core loop:** Read a haunted home's move manifest, discover each item's emotional handling rule, coordinate grips and routes, pack the van, and contain escalating hauntings before time expires.

**Closest comparables:** _Overcooked_ for coordination, _Job Simulator_ for physical comedy, and _Tentacular_ for large-object manipulation.

**How not to clone:** Do not reward random throwing or kitchen-style order fulfillment. Each object needs a learnable personality that changes mass, shape, attachment, or route planning.

**Differentiated interaction:** Furniture is both character and physics rule. Success comes from social coordination and empathy-as-mechanics.

**Originality risk:** **Medium** because physics comedy is crowded; emotional rules must be systematic rather than cosmetic.

### 12. Balloon Courtroom

**Immediate visual:** A witness balloon swears it was “nowhere near the moon,” then involuntarily floats toward a moon-shaped evidence lamp. The player clips a leak and the witness's voice rises in pitch.

**Core loop:** Hear testimony, stage physical evidence, adjust air pressure or tether points, provoke a contradiction, and persuade a jury of tiny spectators before the courtroom drifts apart.

**Closest comparables:** _Phoenix Wright: Ace Attorney_ for contradiction-driven courtroom drama and party deduction games for social interpretation.

**How not to clone:** Avoid text-box testimony, evidence-menu objections, named legal characters, and linear scripted contradictions. Lies should be exposed by buoyancy, volume, leaks, and spatial relationships.

**Differentiated interaction:** Rhetoric has physical pressure. Players cross-examine by constructing experiments around an unreliable inflatable body.

**Originality risk:** **Low** in the reviewed set.

### 13. Microbe Parliament

**Immediate visual:** A living petri dish forms a circular parliament. Photosynthetic microbes raise glowing fronds, predators rattle tooth-like cilia, and the water visibly clouds as negotiations fail.

**Core loop:** Read species needs, propose exchanges, physically seat coalitions near resources, watch the ecosystem simulate one cycle, and renegotiate before collapse.

**Closest comparables:** Tabletop strategy such as _Demeo_ for presentation, ecosystem simulators, and negotiation board games.

**How not to clone:** No fantasy party combat, cards as the primary interaction, or conquest victory. Species should transform shared chemistry, making every treaty materially visible.

**Differentiated interaction:** Diplomacy and ecology are the same system: moving a delegate changes oxygen, acidity, food webs, and political leverage.

**Originality risk:** **Low-medium**.

### 14. Solar Sail Duel

**Immediate visual:** Two mirrored craft circle a miniature sun. Players tilt vast reflective sails; a sweeping light band pushes one craft outward while casting the rival into shadow.

**Core loop:** Predict a moving light field, angle sail facets, bank momentum, steal or deny illumination, and cross scoring gates without projectiles.

**Closest comparables:** _Racket: Nx_ and _Blaston_ for physical competitive intensity, plus sailing games for wind-reading.

**How not to clone:** No guns, dodge bullets, racket strikes, or conventional wind arrows. The shared star and moving occlusion field should be the only source of acceleration.

**Differentiated interaction:** Offense and defense are acts of reflection: catching light moves you, while casting a shadow can strand the opponent.

**Originality risk:** **Low-medium**. The duel format is familiar; solar-light topology is not.

### 15. Library of Falling Words

**Immediate visual:** The word **HEAVY** tumbles from a shelf. The player catches it, sticks it to a paper staircase, and the stairs crash through a floor just before the room folds upward.

**Core loop:** Read a collapsing scene, catch a limited set of physical words, combine noun/property/action fragments, attach them to world objects, and improvise a route before the next structural shift.

**Closest comparables:** _Baba Is You_ for grammar-based rules and action puzzlers for real-time pressure.

**How not to clone:** Avoid grid movement, “X IS Y” syntax, turn-based undo loops, or recreating Baba's property vocabulary. Words should have mass, placement, orientation, and timing in a live 3D space.

**Differentiated interaction:** Language is both rule and falling debris. Catching, carrying, and physically positioning a word is part of solving it.

**Originality risk:** **Medium** because the grammar-puzzle comparison is strong; physical real-time syntax must be central.

### 16. Fossil League Reconstruction

**Immediate visual:** A museum table holds a forked bone bat, a ring with bite marks, and a mural of six-legged athletes. The player assembles a theory, and a ghost stadium materializes to test it.

**Core loop:** Examine artifacts, infer two or three rules, place those rules on a match board, play a short version of the reconstructed sport, and revise the theory when the simulation produces contradictions.

**Closest comparables:** Object reconstruction games such as _Puzzling Places_ and sports minigame collections.

**How not to clone:** Do not stop after assembling an artifact or present familiar sports with fantasy skins. The player's inferred rules must generate the playable sport.

**Differentiated interaction:** Deduction becomes performance. A mistaken archaeological interpretation creates a strange but playable match that teaches why the theory is wrong.

**Originality risk:** **Low** in the reviewed set.

### 17. Invisible City Radio

**Immediate visual:** The room is almost empty except for antennas and dust. Turning one antenna brings a train station to life in binaural sound; crossing two signals reveals the outline of an apartment in particles.

**Core loop:** Tune a frequency, triangulate a voice or event, walk/rotate through interference, pin sound landmarks into a map, and connect simultaneous broadcasts into one citywide story.

**Closest comparables:** _Outer Wilds_ for knowledge-led discovery and narrative spatial-audio works.

**How not to clone:** Avoid a solar-system loop, visual planet exploration, or a conventional audio log scavenger hunt. Players should actively locate, combine, and separate live broadcasts.

**Differentiated interaction:** The city is absent visually until understood acoustically. Radio interference is navigation, occlusion, and unreliable narrator.

**Originality risk:** **Low**. Accessibility design must provide captions, waveform direction, and visual equivalents.

### 18. Knotworld Clinic

**Immediate visual:** A tiny river, food chain, and migration route are braided into one luminous knot. Pulling the wrong loop dries a wetland; loosening another lets fish and birds flow again.

**Core loop:** Inspect a sick ecosystem, trace strands to their living endpoints, pass loops through one another without severing them, restore flow, and watch species return.

**Closest comparables:** _Cubism_ and tactile disentanglement puzzles.

**How not to clone:** Avoid fixed polycube assembly and ordinary rope untangling. Every strand should carry a resource or relationship whose changing flow provides feedback.

**Differentiated interaction:** Topology is ecology. The knot's mathematical state and the world's biological health are the same readable object.

**Originality risk:** **Low**.

### 19. Moon Garden Tides

**Immediate visual:** A hand-sized coast surrounds the player. Dragging a moon around it pulls a silver tide across seed beds; one alien flower opens only in the brief overlap of two moon shadows.

**Core loop:** Plant a small set of species, position moons and breakwaters, run a tidal cycle, harvest a new seed trait, and redesign the coast for the next ecological combination.

**Closest comparables:** _Garden of the Sea_ and cozy systemic gardening games.

**How not to clone:** Avoid watering, harvesting, and crafting as generic chores. Growth must be controlled primarily by orbital phase, tide height, salinity, and eclipse timing.

**Differentiated interaction:** The player gardens by editing celestial mechanics. The entire level communicates cause and effect as a moving tide line.

**Originality risk:** **Low-medium**.

### 20. Instrument Inside-Out

**Immediate visual:** A tiny courier surfs along a cello string inside a cathedral-sized instrument. The player pinches the string, changing both its pitch and the hill the courier is climbing.

**Core loop:** Read the approaching musical structure, stretch/shorten strings and air columns, route the courier through resonant gates, and land at the soundboard on the final chord.

**Closest comparables:** _Thumper_ and rhythm traversal games.

**How not to clone:** Avoid a fixed lane, beat-hit obstacles, or abstract violence. The player continuously retunes and reshapes the path; the music is the geometry's audible consequence.

**Differentiated interaction:** Track editing, locomotion, and composition happen together. XR hands manipulate instrument parts; desktop/mobile gestures bend the same curves.

**Originality risk:** **Medium-low** if there are no conventional beat targets.

## Originality and market cautions

- “No close match found” means no close match was identified in this bounded storefront, web, and known-title scan. It is not proof that no similar prototype, jam game, patent, academic project, or unpublished game exists.
- Before selecting a finalist, repeat searches using the final title, mechanic sentence, and key art; perform trademark/domain checks separately.
- Avoid visual identity that closely mimics a comparable even when the mechanic differs. Recognizable silhouettes, UI layouts, color systems, character proportions, and copy can create cloning concerns independently of rules.
- The proposal should describe comparables honestly. “X meets Y” is useful internally, but public marketing should lead with the new player verb.
- A hackathon prototype should demonstrate one complete differentiating loop. Breadth of content is less valuable than proving the concept's unique causal system.
- For any educational claims in Loop Engineer, have a subject-matter expert verify terminology and learning outcomes. Define SDLC accurately, preserve SDLR as the project's unexpanded framework term, and do not invent an SDLR expansion.

### Addendum conclusion

The portfolio remains meaningfully diverse if the five high-collision pairs are separated in both mechanics demonstrations and key art. The greatest market-similarity pressure falls on **Portal Paparazzi**, **Dream Assembly Line**, and **Size Thief** because perspective photography, programming automation, and scale puzzles already have strong audience reference points. Their proposals should lead with, respectively, **framed spaces become briefly contiguous**, **surreal traits expose every matched rule**, and **total size is conserved between donor and receiver**.
