# Marketing and copy strategy: Kaiju QA Devpost video

## Executive recommendation

Lead with the problem judges already recognize from AI-assisted development:
making a change is fast; proving that the change did not break something else is
the hard part. Let the baby kaiju make that problem funny and visual.

**Recommended cold-open voice-over:**

> AI can make a change in seconds. Can you prove it did not break the city?

Cut immediately from that question to the fragile tower falling. Then reveal:

**Recommended title card:**

> **KAIJU QA**  
> *The regression is the real monster.*

**Recommended one-sentence promise:**

> Kaiju QA turns AI-era quality engineering into a three-minute spatial game:
> stage an edge case, inspect the evidence, fix the regression, and earn the
> right to ship.

**Recommended closing voice-over:**

> Anyone can make the change. A Loop Engineer proves it deserves to ship.

**Recommended end-card CTA:**

> **Catch the regression. Earn the release.**

If the video contains mock scenes rather than matching game capture, keep a
small but readable **CONCEPT VISUALIZATION** label on every applicable shot.
Do not hide the disclosure on only the final card.

## Positioning hierarchy

The video should communicate these ideas in this order:

1. **Recognizable problem:** AI accelerates change, but release judgment still
   depends on evidence.
2. **Memorable fantasy:** the learner is the quality engineer for a helpful baby
   kaiju, not a programmer fighting a monster.
3. **Playable insight:** a happy-path pass is not coverage; a broad fix can
   create a regression; a targeted change must preserve every criterion.
4. **Earned payoff:** the full suite passes and the release stamp is deserved.
5. **Credible process and impact:** the lesson is designed as a short,
   cross-device practice loop, with the Codex collaboration shown through real
   planning, implementation, testing, and review evidence.

Do not begin by defining “Loop Engineering.” Show the tower, the ambulance, and
the evidence first. Name the practice only after the audience understands the
decision it describes.

### Recommended opening sequence

Use the top two ideas as a sequence rather than forcing one line to do every
job:

- **0:00–0:04:** “AI can make a change in seconds.” Show the baby kaiju moving
  confidently through the tabletop city.
- **0:04–0:08:** “Can you prove it did not break the city?” The fragile tower
  falls with a warm, compact comic beat.
- **0:08–0:12:** Title card: “KAIJU QA — The regression is the real monster.”
- **0:12–0:17:** Deliver the one-sentence promise over the test district reset.

This satisfies the plan's requirement to state the problem and promise within
the first ten seconds while preserving a clean title reveal.

## Beat-to-judge-value map

The official criteria are equally weighted, but the video should never announce
“now for Technological Implementation” or display rubric headings. Each story
beat should let judges infer the strength from visible proof.

| Time | Story beat and suggested copy | What the judge should infer naturally |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 0:00–0:12 | Tower failure, opening question, title/tagline | **Quality of the Idea:** a novel, instantly legible quality-engineering fantasy. **Potential Impact:** it addresses the verification gap created by faster AI-assisted change. |
| 0:12–0:25 | Stalled-car happy path. “The kaiju helps the car. That is a pass—but only for the test we remembered to run.” | **Design:** the learning rule is embedded in one simple action and readable result. **Implementation:** deterministic scenarios and explicit pass evidence are part of the mechanic, not narration pasted onto it. |
| 0:25–0:39 | Place the fragile-tower scenario and rerun. “Add one edge case, keep the behavior, and the happy path becomes a useful failure.” | **Design:** test coverage is made spatial and tactile. **Quality:** the player stages evidence instead of answering a quiz or typing a prompt. |
| 0:39–0:54 | Apply the broad freeze rule; tower passes, ambulance fails. “One fix saved the tower—and trapped the ambulance.” | **Quality:** the memorable comic reversal is also the core systems insight. **Potential Impact:** learners practice preserving prior criteria rather than optimizing for the newest failure. |
| 0:54–1:09 | Compare retained paths and place the targeted slow-zone boundary. “The evidence points to the smallest safe change.” | **Implementation:** persistent traces, scenario state, and a bounded state transition make the reasoning inspectable. Show this rather than spending time on a stack list. |
| 1:09–1:20 | Run all scenarios; green/shape-coded results; tiny release seal. “Now every scenario passes. Release confidence is earned, not assumed.” | **Design:** satisfying feedback and a clear gate. **Quality:** the release stamp turns an abstract engineering decision into a toy-like payoff. |
| 1:20–1:29 | Fast cross-device and Codex-evidence montage | **Implementation:** one semantic loop across mouse, touch, and XR, plus concrete Codex artifacts such as acceptance criteria, tests, review findings, and fixes. Only show claims backed by the final repository. |
| 1:29–1:35 | Classroom/replay image and end card | **Potential Impact:** a short practice activity for learners and instructors, not an unsupported promise of mastery. Close on “Anyone can make the change. A Loop Engineer proves it deserves to ship.” |

### Evidence overlays that sell without reciting the rubric

Keep overlays short enough to read in roughly one second:

- `HAPPY PATH: PASS`
- `NEW EDGE CASE`
- `TOWER: PASS  /  AMBULANCE: FAIL`
- `COMPARE THE EVIDENCE`
- `TARGETED CHANGE`
- `FULL REGRESSION: PASS`
- `RELEASE EARNED`
- `MOUSE • TOUCH • XR` — only if matching behavior is verified
- `BRIEF → TESTS → REVIEW → EVIDENCE` — only with matching repository proof

Use shape, position, motion, and words in addition to red/green. The marketing
benefit is not merely accessibility compliance: judges can understand the
state change even on a small embedded player or muted social preview.

## Voice and copy direction

- Read with amused confidence, not trailer-boom drama. The kaiju is helpful and
  the failure is useful; nobody is mocked for getting a test wrong.
- Prefer concrete nouns: **tower, ambulance, path, boundary, evidence, release**.
  Avoid abstract clusters such as “iterative agentic lifecycle optimization.”
- Let the comedy come from scale and consequence: a tiny approval stamp, a
  proud kaiju, and an over-broad fix with an obviously bad side effect.
- Keep voice-over sentences under about 15 words during action-heavy shots.
- Do not repeat every caption in the narration. Captions should transcribe the
  voice-over; separate overlays should label state and evidence.
- Introduce “Loop Engineer” as the identity the player earns, not as terminology
  they must memorize before playing.

## Truthfulness and claim guardrails

- Say **“built with Codex and GPT-5.6”** only when the final video can show the
  corresponding repository evidence and the submission meets the official
  requirement. Otherwise use present-tense design language and keep the concept
  disclosure visible.
- Do not imply a live generative model controls the kaiju if outcomes are a
  deterministic state machine. Determinism is a strength here: it makes tests
  repeatable and causality teachable.
- Do not claim measured learning gains, “mastery,” production safety, or
  classroom adoption without a real study or pilot. Claim a short practice
  opportunity for verification and release judgment.
- Do not call the game a coding lesson. The distinctive promise is practicing
  acceptance criteria, evidence, regression protection, and a release gate.
- Avoid “fully autonomous,” “built entirely by AI,” or a generic sped-up coding
  montage. Show the human/Codex collaboration boundary through decisions,
  acceptance criteria, checks, rejected ideas, and independent review.
- Avoid “break nothing,” “bug-free,” or “guaranteed safe.” The lesson is to make
  risk visible and earn the next decision, not eliminate uncertainty.
