### Eligibility

The official rules permit the following entrants, subject to all exclusions:

- Individuals who have reached the age of majority where they reside.
- Residents of countries or territories that support OpenAI API access and are
  not otherwise excluded.
- Parents or guardians of students under 18, or under the local age of
  majority.
- Teams of eligible individuals.
- Organizations already organized or incorporated in an OpenAI API-supported
  country or territory, including companies and nonprofit entities.

An eligible individual may participate on multiple teams or organizations and
may also submit individually. A team or organization must appoint an eligible
representative authorized to submit on its behalf.[R3]

The rules exclude:

- Individuals or organizations outside the incorporated OpenAI API-supported
  geography list.[C1]
- Places where U.S. or local law prohibits participation or prize receipt. The
  rules explicitly name Brazil, Quebec, Russia, Crimea, Cuba, Iran, North Korea,
  and Syria, plus other territories designated by the U.S. Treasury's Office of
  Foreign Assets Control.
- Sponsor/administrator promotion entities; their employees, representatives,
  agents, immediate family, and qualifying household members; people involved
  in producing or administering the hackathon; judges and their employers;
  relevant parent/subsidiary/affiliate organizations; and any entrant creating
  a real or apparent conflict of interest.[R3]

Because the supported-country page is a live list, the entrant and any team
representative should verify eligibility again on submission day.[C1]

### Required build and tracks

The challenge asks entrants to create a project using **Codex with GPT-5.6**.
Eligible forms include apps, agents, websites, games, workflows, backend tools,
and plugins, provided the project fits one selected category.[O1][R4]

| Track | Official scope |
| --- | --- |
| Apps for Your Life | Consumer apps for everyday life, including productivity, creativity, home, family, travel, health, and personal finance |
| Work & Productivity | Tools that improve team speed or effectiveness, including workflow automation, support, analytics, sales, and back-office work |
| Developer Tools | Developer-facing testing, DevOps, agentic workflow, and security tools |
| Education | Projects that advance AI for students, teachers, or educational organizations |

Projects must run consistently on their intended platform and work as depicted
in the submission video and description. A new project must be created during
the Submission Period. A pre-existing project must be meaningfully extended
with Codex and/or GPT-5.6 after **July 13, 2026 at 9:00 a.m. PT**; only the new
work is evaluated. The entrant must distinguish old from new work with evidence
such as dated commits or timestamped Codex sessions. Third-party SDKs, APIs,
data, and assets must be used under valid authorization and licenses.[R4]

### Required submission packet

The entrant must complete the required Devpost submission fields during the
Submission Period and provide the following:[O1][R4][RS]

1. **Working project** built with Codex using GPT-5.6 and satisfying the project
   requirements.
2. **One category** that best aligns with the project.
3. **Text description** explaining the project's features and functionality.
4. **Demonstration video** meeting every video constraint below.
5. **Code repository URL** meeting every repository constraint below.
6. **README** with setup instructions, sample data if needed, clear run guidance,
   and an account of the team's collaboration with Codex. It should identify
   where Codex accelerated work, where humans made key product/engineering/design
   decisions, and how GPT-5.6 and Codex contributed to the result.
7. **`/feedback` Codex Session ID** for the project thread in which the majority
   of the core functionality was built.
8. For a plugin or developer tool, **installation instructions, supported
   platforms, and a judge-ready demo/sandbox/test account** that does not
   require rebuilding from scratch.

All submitted materials must be in English, or include English translations of
the video, description, testing instructions, and other materials.[RS]

### Video constraints

- The video must be **shorter than three minutes**. Judges do not have to watch
  beyond three minutes.
- It must visibly demonstrate the working project.
- It must contain audio explaining both what was built and how **Codex and
  GPT-5.6** were used.
- It must be uploaded to YouTube and be publicly visible, with the URL entered
  in the submission form.
- It may not contain third-party trademarks, copyrighted music, or other
  copyrighted material without permission.[RS]

### Repository and README constraints

- A repository URL is required for judging and testing.
- A public repository must carry relevant licensing.
- A private repository must be shared with both `testing@devpost.com` and
  `build-week-event@openai.com`.
- The README must make setup and use reproducible and explain the actual Codex
  collaboration, acceleration, human decisions, and GPT-5.6/Codex contribution.
- For a pre-existing codebase, the repository documentation should make the
  post-July-13 work boundary unambiguous.[O1][R4][RS]

### Testing and access constraints

- Judges must receive access to a working website, functioning demo, or test
  build. A private website must include login credentials in its testing
  instructions.
- The project must remain free of charge and unrestricted for Sponsor,
  Administrator, and judge testing through **August 5, 2026 at 5:00 p.m. PT**.
- Judges are not required to run the project; they may evaluate only the text,
  images, and video.
- For software dependent on proprietary or uncommon third-party hardware other
  than smartphones, tablets, or desktop computers, the Sponsor/Administrator
  may request physical access to the hardware.
- The submission must be original and entrant-owned, respect third-party IP and
  open-source licenses, and contain no malicious code.[R4][RS]

An entrant may submit multiple projects, but each must be unique and
substantially different from the entrant's other submissions.[RS]

### Judging process and equally weighted criteria

Stage One is pass/fail: the project must show baseline viability, reasonably fit
the challenge theme, and reasonably apply the required hackathon APIs/SDKs.
Stage Two evaluates every passing submission on four **equally weighted**
criteria.[R7]

| Criterion | What judges evaluate |
| --- | --- |
| Technological Implementation | Thorough, skillful Codex use; genuine engineering effort; a working, non-trivial implementation |
| Design | A runnable, complete, coherent product experience rather than only a technical proof of concept |
| Potential Impact | A specific, credible real problem and audience, with demonstrated evidence that the solution addresses them |
| Quality of the Idea | Creativity, novelty, and meaningful differentiation from existing concepts |

Tie-breaking follows the criteria in listed order, making **Technological
Implementation** the first tie-breaker, followed by Design, Potential Impact,
and Quality of the Idea. The rules allow one or more judging rounds and permit
expert panels, peer review, automated AI-assisted analysis, or a combination of
methods.[R7]

## Recommendations

### Winning Education-track position

Submit the selected runnable **Loop Engineer game**, not the five-concept book,
as the product. Position it as an experiential learning tool for a precise
audience rather than as a general-purpose game or a showcase of WebXR.

**Recommended tagline**

> Turn the AI build-test-learn loop into muscle memory.

**Recommended one-sentence pitch**

> Loop Engineer is a three-minute cross-device learning game where aspiring
> developers practice a complete AI-assisted software feedback loop, see the
> consequences of their decisions, and improve through an immediate retry.

**Message house**

- **Primary learner:** first-year computer-science/software-engineering students,
  bootcamp learners, and career switchers. Secondary user: instructors seeking
  a short, replayable class activity or formative assessment.
- **Specific problem:** AI can accelerate code generation while leaving novice
  developers without the judgment to define success, inspect results, diagnose
  failure, and iterate. Static SDLC diagrams and quizzes test vocabulary more
  readily than decision-making.
- **Product promise:** in under three minutes, the learner completes one full
  loop, experiences a visible consequence, and gets a second attempt that
  rewards better reasoning.
- **Education proof:** make the learning objective, learner decision, feedback,
  retry, and improved outcome visible inside the demo. Prefer an observable
  before/after choice or micro-rubric over an unsupported claim that the game
  improves learning.
- **Differentiator:** this is an embodied systems-thinking simulation that turns
  an abstract AI-era development process into spatial, consequential play. It
  is not another chatbot tutor, flash-card quiz, or passive explainer.
- **Distribution story:** one browser codebase reaches classrooms on desktop
  and mobile while offering an optional WebXR embodiment. XR is an enhancement,
  not a gate to judging or adoption.
- **Track discipline:** select **Education** only. Describe the learner and
  instructor value first; treat the developer/game technology as the enabling
  mechanism, not as a competing Developer Tools pitch.

The integrated proposal should use the learning-loop vocabulary selected by the
learning-design specialist. Until that is fixed, a safe provisional sequence is
**frame intent → build → observe/test → diagnose → revise/retry**.

### Judging-criteria story

Treat equal weighting as four one-quarter story obligations, while giving the
first tie-break criterion extra visibility.

| Criterion | Submission story | Evidence to put on screen or in the repository |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Technological Implementation | Codex and GPT-5.6 enabled a non-trivial, cross-device IWSDK game, but humans owned learning goals, scope, and final decisions | Working build; architecture diagram; tests; dated commits; selected Codex session evidence; precise human/agent decision log |
| Design | A learner can understand the goal, act, fail or succeed, learn, and retry within one coherent three-minute experience | Uncut core-loop footage; clear onboarding; readable feedback; desktop/touch/XR parity; accessibility and comfort notes |
| Potential Impact | A named learner population needs practice making feedback-loop decisions, and instructors need a short deployable activity | One explicit learning objective; classroom use case; lightweight assessment signal; honest pilot/usability observations if available |
| Quality of the Idea | Software iteration becomes a physical game system with consequences, rather than a lecture, quiz, or generic AI tutor | Comparable-product contrast; distinctive mechanic; one memorable visual metaphor; explanation of why the mechanic teaches the concept |

Do not claim runtime GPT-5.6 functionality unless the shipped game actually uses
it at runtime. The mandatory story can instead be a rigorously evidenced account
of how GPT-5.6 and Codex were used to build, test, and refine the project.

### Demo-video strategy

Target **2:40–2:50**, leaving safety margin under the official three-minute
ceiling. Use captions in addition to required audio, show the product by 0:10,
and make the video sufficient for a judge who never opens the build.

| Time | Beat |
| --- | --- |
| 0:00–0:10 | Visual hook and one-line learner problem |
| 0:10–0:25 | Name the learner, learning objective, and Education-track fit |
| 0:25–1:30 | Mostly uninterrupted play showing the core decision loop and feedback |
| 1:30–1:55 | Show a failure or weak outcome, diagnosis, retry, and improved result |
| 1:55–2:15 | Show desktop and touch access, then optional XR embodiment; mention accessibility/comfort |
| 2:15–2:40 | Show repository/session evidence and narrate exactly how Codex and GPT-5.6 accelerated the work and where humans decided |
| 2:40–2:50 | State classroom impact, differentiation, and final title card with demo/repository availability |

Avoid title-card-heavy editing, a long team introduction, sped-up code montages,
or claims unsupported by visible gameplay. Use only cleared music/assets; silence
under narration is safer than questionable background music.

### Judge-access strategy for WebXR

- Make the desktop browser path the default judge URL and ensure the complete
  learning loop is playable there without an account, purchase, headset, or
  special browser configuration.
- Offer mobile/touch and WebXR as clearly labeled alternate modes.
- Put a 30-second quick-start and tested-browser matrix at the top of the README.
- Keep the deployment live and free through the end of judging, not merely
  through the submission deadline.
- If a headset-only behavior cannot be reproduced on desktop, show it clearly
  in the public video and document the exact hardware/browser tested.
- Never place reusable personal credentials in a public README. If private test
  credentials are necessary, use a dedicated limited test account and enter the
  credentials only where Devpost's testing instructions permit.

Maintain one master Markdown document, then adapt its blocks to the Devpost
form, README, narration, and project page.

1. **Submission identity**

   - 25-word pitch

3. **What Loop Engineer does**
   - One-paragraph product description
   - Core player verb
   - Three-minute experience arc
   - Learning objective and observable evidence
4. **Feature list**
   - Core loop and retry
   - Feedback/assessment mechanism
   - Desktop, touch, and WebXR modes
   - Accessibility, comfort, and reduced-motion provisions

   - Closest familiar alternatives
   - Mechanic-level distinction
   - Why embodiment/interactivity improves this specific lesson
6. **How it was built**

   - Major systems and data flow
   - Testing and performance approach
   - Third-party dependencies, assets, and licenses
7. **How Codex and GPT-5.6 were used**
   - Chronological collaboration summary
   - Tasks Codex accelerated
   - Key human product, learning, design, and engineering decisions
   - Important rejected suggestions or corrections
   - Session ID and dated commit/test evidence
8. **Challenges, accomplishments, and learning**
   - Hardest technical and product constraints
   - What was cut and why
   - What the team learned from testing

   - Honest current evidence
   - Classroom deployment scenario
   - Lightweight pilot/evaluation plan
   - Next build step without inflating the submitted scope
10. **Judge access**
    - Public demo URL
    - One-sentence quick-start
    - Tested platforms/browsers
    - Test account instructions if required

11. **Repository compliance**
    - Repository URL and visibility
    - License
    - Setup, sample data, and run instructions
    - New-versus-pre-existing work boundary
    - Dependency/asset attribution
12. **Media package**
    - Public YouTube URL and final duration
    - Hero image with title and learning promise
    - Desktop gameplay screenshot
    - Mobile gameplay screenshot
    - XR screenshot if available
    - Failure/feedback/retry screenshot that proves the learning loop
13. **Final submission checklist**
    - Category selected
    - Description complete
    - Video public, under three minutes, audible, and rights-cleared
    - Demo accessible and free through August 5 at 5:00 p.m. PT
    - Repository licensed or shared with both required email addresses
    - README reproducible and Codex collaboration documented
    - `/feedback` session ID entered
    - English text/captions/translations complete
    - IP, asset, SDK, API, and data rights verified
    - Representative and country eligibility rechecked

## Unresolved items to confirm before submission

1. **Live form fields and limits:** neither the overview nor rules state every
   Devpost field, character limit, cover-image requirement, screenshot count,
   or accepted image dimensions. Inspect the live submission form before final
   copy and media export.
2. **“Pro Account” fulfillment:** the rules do not clarify the exact product,
   number of accounts per team, activation date, or transferability beyond the
   general non-transferability language.
3. **Codex session aggregation:** the rules request one `/feedback` Session ID
   where most core functionality was built. If implementation spans multiple
   agent sessions, deliberately choose a canonical build thread and document
   supporting sessions in the README.
4. **Pre-existing baseline:** this repository predates the submission work.
   Record the exact pre-July-13 baseline commit and identify only eligible new
   work; confirm with the orchestrator before making claims.
5. **WebXR hardware access:** the rules reserve the right to request physical
   access for uncommon proprietary/third-party hardware. Confirm whether the
   final experience has any headset-only requirement; a complete desktop path
   materially reduces this risk but does not waive the rule.
6. **Dynamic eligibility:** verify the entrant, representative, and organization
   against the current supported-country list and exclusions on July 21.
7. **Submission readiness:** the current branch is a docs-only design spike.
   The concept book is not a substitute for the required working project,
   public video, test access, repository evidence, and session ID.
