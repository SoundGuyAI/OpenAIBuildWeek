# Narrative directions — Loop Engineer

Logical assignment: `DISC-NAR-01`

Role: Narrative creative director

Deliverable: Five distinct narrative fantasies for a short, single-player Loop
Engineer session.

## Narrative design contract

The fiction should motivate the learning loop, never replace it. Every concept
below keeps the same readable story grammar:

`understand the goal -> inspect the current system -> make one scoped change -> run a test -> read visible evidence -> adapt -> resolve or retry`

- The first actionable problem is visible within 20 seconds, and the first
  useful test result arrives within 45 seconds.
- A failed run is framed as information, not punishment. Retry is immediate and
  preserves or overlays the evidence from the previous attempt.
- Requirements, test status, and causality remain visible in the world. Lore is
  optional texture; it is never required to discover the next valid action.
- Each concept fits one compact, mostly stationary diorama or workbench. Required
  actions reduce to point/select, drag/place or rotate, and activate/test: mouse
  on desktop, tap/drag on mobile, and ray or direct grab in XR.
- Essential story information is communicated with shape, motion, spatial
  change, icons, and short captions. Audio and color can reinforce but never
  carry the only cue.
- The exact instructional vocabulary and assessment labels should be reconciled
  with `DISC-LD-01`. The narrative requirement is that the loop remains visibly
  causal even if those labels change.

## 1. Loop Engineer: Stormglass

**One-line premise:** In a shattered sky-lighthouse, iterate a
weather-routing machine until it opens one safe corridor for a courier trapped
inside a living storm.

**Player role:** The newly arrived stormkeeper taking over an unfinished repair,
with evidence left by earlier keepers but no all-knowing guide.

**Stakes:** The courier needs a stable route before the storm closes around the
harbor. Failed attempts are predictive test pulses, so no person is harmed while
the player learns.

**Environmental storytelling:** Cracked stormglass, patched brass housings, and
old calibration marks show years of practical maintenance. A route chart above
the workbench shows the courier circling offshore. Each test leaves a brief
ghost trail through the clouds, making the difference between attempts visible
without opening a log screen.

**How the educational loop stays legible:** The target corridor is the visible
requirement; a prism or routing module is the scoped change; pulling the test
lever runs the same storm pulse; lightning traces expose the first mismatch;
the next attempt can be compared directly with the last. Opening the corridor
is the release, not a separate cutscene reward.

**Beginning / escalation / ending beats:**

- **Beginning, 0:00–0:45:** A courier flare wakes the dark lighthouse. One dead
  lens and one matching socket pulse in turn. The player installs or aligns the
  lens, runs a test, and clears the nearest cloud ring for a first success.
- **Escalation, 0:45–2:15:** The full route reveals two linked faults. Fixing the
  obvious overload bends the corridor into a crosswind, so the player reads the
  new trace and changes one variable rather than rebuilding blindly. A safe,
  longer route and a tighter, less tolerant route offer a small strategy choice.
- **Ending, 2:15–3:00:** The final repeated test remains stable. The player
  commits the route, the cloud layers iris open, and the courier passes through
  the lighthouse beam into harbor. The route board records the chosen solution
  and offers the alternate route as the replay hook.

**Judge-retellable moment:** A failed pulse paints the exact fault across the
storm in branching lightning; after one informed prism change, the same test
turns that scar into a luminous tunnel and the courier dives through it. The
one-sentence retell is: “I used the storm’s own failure trace to debug a
lighthouse and fly a ship home.”

**Tone guardrails:** Heroic competence, not disaster trauma. The courier never
crashes on failure. Avoid dense electrical or nautical jargon, prophecy, and a
narrator who gives away the fix. Lightning should be beautiful and readable,
not strobing or frightening. The player is a skilled maintainer, not a chosen
one.

**Cross-platform interaction fit:** All manipulation happens around one large
lens table with generous sockets and a single test lever/button. No locomotion,
two-handed action, voice input, or high-speed aiming is required.

## 2. Loop Engineer: The Tomorrow Garden

**One-line premise:** Run accelerated seasons inside a seedship terrarium,
iterating its care plan until a tiny alien habitat can sustain itself at dawn.

**Player role:** A cycle gardener preparing one ark pod for landing, equal parts
caretaker and systems observer.

**Stakes:** The ship needs one verified, stable habitat before it reaches the new
world. Unsuccessful growth cycles are reversible projections; the living seed
stock remains safe.

**Environmental storytelling:** Growth rings are etched into the circular
bench, while pictorial tags record earlier light, water, and pollination trials.
Translucent silhouettes show where roots and leaves reached in prior cycles.
Beyond the glass, the destination planet slowly rises, making the deadline
visible without a timer dominating the scene.

**How the educational loop stays legible:** The habitat card displays concrete
survival criteria. The player changes one care input, starts a compressed season,
and watches water, roots, and pollinators move through the same cycle. End-state
shapes and short indicators show which criterion passed, regressed, or remained
untested. Previous gardens stay as ghost overlays for comparison.

**Beginning / escalation / ending beats:**

- **Beginning, 0:00–0:45:** A single folded sprout leans toward an unpowered sun
  tile. The player supplies the missing input and runs one season; the sprout
  roots, opens, and produces the first clear pass marker.
- **Escalation, 0:45–2:15:** A second species and a pollinator wake, exposing a
  dependency the first success did not test. More water helps one plant but
  floods the other, so the player uses root and flow traces to make a controlled
  adjustment instead of maximizing every input.
- **Ending, 2:15–3:00:** The final season completes with no intervention. The
  terrarium shifts from a set of parts into a tiny self-sustaining landscape,
  the ark pod seals with a verified-cycle emblem, and the landing light arrives.

**Judge-retellable moment:** The player briefly overlays three imperfect ghost
gardens, spots the one dependency they share, and starts a final season. A
single sprout grows around the whole bench into a luminous miniature habitat as
the planet fills the window. The retell is: “I learned from three failed seasons
and grew a world that could run on its own.”

**Tone guardrails:** Cozy science fantasy, not ecological guilt or domination of
nature. Avoid random outcomes: every visible result must follow a readable
input. No dying animals, wilted-body horror, or moral scolding. Do not imply
that a complex real ecosystem is solved by three sliders; this is an explicitly
stylized training terrarium.

**Cross-platform interaction fit:** The entire habitat is a waist-height
diorama. Inputs are large tiles or tokens that can be clicked, dragged, tapped,
or grabbed, followed by one prominent “run season” control. Observation rewards
depth in XR but never requires leaning or walking.

## 3. Loop Engineer: Galactic Test Kitchen

**One-line premise:** Debug a universal food printer by prototyping one welcome
dish that satisfies three nonhuman guests’ visible needs before first-contact
dinner.

**Player role:** The station’s systems chef, responsible for turning clear guest
requirements into a reliable recipe pipeline rather than guessing what “alien
food” should be.

**Stakes:** A good shared meal opens the station’s first friendly gathering; a
bad sample causes awkward reactions and useful feedback, never poisoning,
starvation, or interstellar war.

**Environmental storytelling:** Rejected sample trays carry consistent reaction
stickers, the printer bears charming layers of field repairs, and transparent
tubes expose each recipe stage. Guest silhouettes wait behind the dining dome,
and an inherited apron covered in small acceptance stamps suggests many earlier
iterations by many cooks.

**How the educational loop stays legible:** Guest requirement cards are visible
before the first build. Ingredient, process, and presentation modules form a
short pipeline. A sample run produces both a dish and a reaction panel showing
which requirements passed, conflicted, or were not exercised. The final batch
button remains locked until the same recipe passes a repeat test.

**Beginning / escalation / ending beats:**

- **Beginning, 0:00–0:45:** One guest presents three simple pictograms. The
  printer highlights one empty pipeline slot. Filling it and printing a sample
  earns two enthusiastic checks and one precise “too warm” response.
- **Escalation, 0:45–2:15:** The other guests reveal needs that overlap but do
  not perfectly agree. A flashy new module introduces a regression, prompting
  the player to compare reactions and make the smallest recipe change that
  satisfies all three rather than restarting the kitchen.
- **Ending, 2:15–3:00:** A repeat sample passes. The player releases the batch,
  the dining dome opens, and the recipe travels visibly through the printer in
  three synchronized stages before landing at the shared table.

**Judge-retellable moment:** A flawed sample splits into a clean visual report of
three unmet needs; one informed module swap turns the next batch into luminous
noodles that curl into the guests’ shared constellation as all three stamp the
recipe accepted. The retell is: “I debugged dinner from alien user feedback and
shipped a recipe everyone could share.”

**Tone guardrails:** Warm workplace comedy, not frantic chaos. Never mock real
cuisines, accents, bodies, dietary restrictions, or the guests’ communication.
Avoid gross-out food, allergy danger, and “misunderstanding causes war” stakes.
Jokes may decorate feedback but cannot hide or contradict a requirement. The
guests’ responses must be consistent enough to learn from.

**Cross-platform interaction fit:** A single counter holds a few large pipeline
modules, sample and release controls, and a stable reaction panel. Every action
maps cleanly to click/drag, tap/drag, XR ray, or direct grab; there is no chopping
rhythm, precision pouring, or simultaneous-hand requirement.

## 4. Loop Engineer: Sunrise Express

**One-line premise:** In a city that receives daylight by rail, plan, test, and
dispatch one “release train” through a broken orbital switchyard before morning.

**Player role:** The midnight release conductor responsible for validating the
route before committing the city’s sunrise.

**Stakes:** A successful dispatch gives every district a full morning. Failed
routes run first as miniature ghost trains on the rehearsal track, so no
passengers or city systems are endangered.

**Environmental storytelling:** The dark city curves below the dispatch table.
Old tickets are stamped with concrete causes such as missed station, blocked
dependency, or late arrival. Photographs show different dawns delivered by past
conductors, while locked track sections physically connect each car’s dependency
to a station requirement.

**How the educational loop stays legible:** The timetable is the acceptance
contract, track switches are scoped implementation choices, and the ghost train
is a repeatable integration test. Its path remains suspended above the rails
after stopping, pointing to the first unmet dependency. Only a passing rehearsal
enables the physical dispatch control.

**Beginning / escalation / ending beats:**

- **Beginning, 0:00–0:45:** The player routes a small lamp car through one switch
  to a nearby district, learning that a rehearsal produces a visible path and
  an immediate station result.
- **Escalation, 0:45–2:15:** The full sunrise train adds linked power, color, and
  timing cars, represented by distinct shapes as well as hues. The obvious
  shortcut strands one dependency. The player reads where the rehearsal stopped
  and chooses between a longer tolerant route and a tighter route that needs one
  additional switch change.
- **Ending, 2:15–3:00:** A complete rehearsal unlocks the brass dispatch punch.
  The player commits the release; the train leaves the table, arcs across the
  skyline, and wakes each district in the same verified sequence.

**Judge-retellable moment:** After the final test, every track segment answers in
sequence. The player punches “SHIP,” the tiny model train becomes city-scale
overhead, and its wake paints dawn across the curved skyline. The retell is: “I
debugged a release train, then shipped the sunrise.”

**Tone guardrails:** Kinetic civic fairy tale, not a parody of overtime, crunch,
or transit disaster. Avoid rail jargon beyond route, switch, station, test, and
dispatch. The time pressure should create shape, not accessibility-hostile
panic. No passengers are shown in danger, and a failed rehearsal never darkens
districts that were already lit.

**Cross-platform interaction fit:** The switchyard is a fixed tabletop with
large toggles, track choices, and one dispatch control. A player can complete it
one-handed with pointer, touch, XR ray, or direct manipulation. The skyline
provides scale in XR without requiring locomotion.

## 5. Loop Engineer: The Museum of Almosts

**One-line premise:** Recover an unfinished inventor collective’s final memory
by iterating one impossible machine until each test reveals the next missing
truth.

**Player role:** A night conservator and forensic maker, preserving the history
of a team’s process rather than finishing a lone genius’s destiny.

**Stakes:** At dawn, an unstable archive must close for conservation; completing
and documenting one verified behavior preserves the makers’ shared memory.
Failed tests add evidence to the exhibit and never erase the original artifact.

**Environmental storytelling:** Prototype versions surround the central plinth,
their exhibit labels showing crossed-out assumptions rather than vague lore.
Tool marks, fingerprints, layered sketches, and contributor stamps reveal a
collective history. Each test projects a five-second translucent workshop echo
that points back to a visible part or requirement.

**How the educational loop stays legible:** The exhibit card states the machine’s
intended behavior in one sentence and three icons. The player forms a hypothesis
from the physical evidence, changes one component, and turns the same test crank.
The artifact’s partial behavior and workshop echo identify what improved and
what remains false. The finished display includes a short retrospective of the
attempts, making iteration part of the outcome rather than hiding it.

**Beginning / escalation / ending beats:**

- **Beginning, 0:00–0:45:** Turning the crank projects only half of a paper bird.
  A nearby prototype and its matching wear mark invite one component change.
  The next test completes the bird’s shape but not its flight, delivering a
  concrete first success and a new question.
- **Escalation, 0:45–2:15:** Two plausible fixes produce different partial
  memories. The player overlays the test echoes, finds the assumption both
  prototypes shared, and revises that single point instead of chasing the most
  spectacular fragment.
- **Ending, 2:15–3:00:** The final test sends the paper bird through the room. It
  unfolds into a full-scale holographic workshop where every contributor and
  failed prototype appears in the final blueprint. The player stamps the exhibit
  “verified, with history preserved.”

**Judge-retellable moment:** Three failed prototype echoes align over the plinth;
the player notices their shared mismatch, makes one scoped change, and turns the
crank. The silent museum blossoms into the makers’ living workshop, with every
failure becoming a visible layer of the completed machine. The retell is: “I
solved a mystery by comparing failed prototypes, and the failures became part of
the final exhibit.”

**Tone guardrails:** Wondrous and gently reflective, not grief-heavy or opaque.
Memory fragments last no more than five seconds and never interrupt control.
Avoid haunted-house cues, chosen-one mythology, and the lone-genius trope.
Failed prototypes are respected, not mocked. Poetic imagery cannot replace a
clear requirement, trace, or next action.

**Cross-platform interaction fit:** The whole experience centers on one plinth,
three large components, ghost overlays, and a test crank/button. Direct grab is
optional; desktop, touch, and XR ray interaction can express the same actions
and receive the same evidence.

| Concept | Dominant fantasy | Emotional arc | Learning emphasis | Spatial signature |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Stormglass | Heroic weather maintenance | Urgency to mastery to relief | Trace a failure and make the smallest corrective change | Vertical lighthouse lens inside a storm |

## Integration assumptions and risks

- The provisional learning spine is the repository’s existing
  `notice -> decide -> act -> read feedback -> adapt -> resolve/retry` loop,
  expanded here to expose goal, test, and release. `DISC-LD-01` owns the final
  sourced teaching model and may rename these steps.
- These directions specify narrative affordances, not final mechanics. The
  integrator should pair each fantasy with the game-design concept whose core
  verb makes the stated test evidence most natural.
- Environmental storytelling can exceed a four-hour build if treated as unique
  props. The scope floor for every concept is one hero machine, one backdrop,
  three decals or cards, and state changes on reused geometry.
- Urgent stakes can accidentally teach speed over observation. Countdown cues
  should pause during reading and interaction, or behave as dramatic progress
  rather than a hard fail timer.
- The hero transformation must be deterministic, readable without audio, and
  short enough to capture in a judged demo. If performance or asset scope is at
  risk, preserve the before/after state change and cut secondary particles.
